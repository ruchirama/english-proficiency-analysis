1. Introduction

English language proficiency is a crucial factor for academic success, particularly in higher education institutions where English is the medium of instruction. In multilingual countries like India, where both native and international students pursue their education, understanding any potential differences in English proficiency can help in designing more effective language support programs.

This report presents a comprehensive statistical analysis addressing the research question: Is there a significant difference in English language proficiency between foreign and Indian students based on their country of origin?

The analysis is based on survey data collected from 100 students (50 Indian and 50 foreign) studying at various Indian universities. The data includes self-reported proficiency ratings across four language skills: reading, listening, speaking, and writing.

Key Research Objectives

  • Determine if there is a statistically significant difference in overall English proficiency between Indian and foreign students
  • Analyze potential differences in specific language skill areas (reading, listening, speaking, writing)
  • Examine if any observed differences vary by level of study (undergraduate vs. postgraduate)
  • Assess the practical significance of any detected differences

2. Literature Review

English language proficiency among university students has been extensively studied due to its critical role in academic success. This section reviews key literature relevant to understanding proficiency differences between native and non-native English speakers in higher education settings.

2.1 English as a Medium of Instruction (EMI)

The expansion of English as a medium of instruction (EMI) in higher education has been documented by numerous studies (Dearden, 2014; Macaro et al., 2018). In multilingual contexts like India, the implementation of EMI presents unique challenges and opportunities, as students come from diverse linguistic backgrounds (Annamalai, 2004; Ramanathan, 2016).

Research by Coleman (2006) and Kirkpatrick (2011) has shown that proficiency gaps can exist between students from different linguistic backgrounds when studying in EMI environments. However, these studies primarily focused on European and East Asian contexts, with limited research specifically comparing Indian and foreign students studying in Indian universities.

2.2 Self-assessment of Language Proficiency

Self-assessment of language proficiency has been validated as a reliable measure in multiple studies (Ross, 1998; Oscarson, 2013). While some variance exists between self-assessment and standardized testing, research has shown moderate to strong correlations between them (Blanche & Merino, 1989; LeBlanc & Painchaud, 1985).

Of particular relevance to our study, Matsuno (2009) and Butler (2018) found that self-assessment across different language skills (reading, writing, listening, speaking) can reveal important patterns in learners' perceived strengths and weaknesses, often with high ecological validity in academic contexts.

2.3 Differential Proficiency Across Language Skills

Previous research has consistently shown that language learners often display differential proficiency across various language skills. Huang (2010) and Yamashita (2013) found that reading skills typically develop earlier and more robustly than productive skills like speaking and writing. For many learners in EFL (English as a Foreign Language) contexts, speaking is often the most challenging skill to develop (Boonkit, 2010; Gan, 2012).

In the Indian context specifically, Sheorey (2006) and Sinha (2009) observed that many Indian students exhibit stronger reading and writing skills compared to speaking skills, often attributed to the examination-oriented education system that has traditionally emphasized literacy over oracy.

2.4 Impact of Educational Level on Proficiency

The relationship between educational level and language proficiency has been explored in various contexts. Longitudinal studies by Sasaki (2007) and Olsen (2017) have demonstrated that language proficiency generally increases with academic progression. However, Trenkic and Warmington (2019) found that the gap between native and non-native speakers may persist or even widen at higher educational levels.

Research specifically examining Indian higher education by Agnihotri and Khanna (1997) and more recently by Bhattacharya (2013) suggests that the transition from undergraduate to postgraduate studies may present additional linguistic challenges, particularly for students from non-English medium backgrounds.

2.5 Research Gap

Despite the extensive literature on English language proficiency in higher education, there remains a notable gap in empirical research comparing Indian and international student populations within Indian universities. Most existing studies have either focused on single population groups or have been conducted in Western or East Asian contexts.

Our study addresses this gap by directly comparing Indian and foreign students in Indian university settings, applying robust statistical methods to assess not just whether differences exist, but also their practical significance and the nuances across different language skills and educational levels.

Relevance to Current Study

The literature review guides our research in several ways:

  • It establishes the validity of using self-assessment measures for language proficiency
  • It provides a theoretical framework for examining differential proficiency across language skills
  • It highlights the importance of considering educational level as a potential moderator
  • It reveals a significant research gap our study aims to address through direct comparison of Indian and international students in Indian university contexts

4. Methodology

4.1 Data Collection

The data was collected through a survey administered to 100 students studying at various Indian universities. The participants included 50 Indian students and 50 foreign students from various countries.

4.2 Measures

English proficiency was measured using self-rated proficiency scores across four key language skills, each rated on a 5-point scale:

  • Reading Comprehension: Understanding academic text (1=Very Weak to 5=Very Strong)
  • Listening Skills: Understanding lectures and spoken English (1=Very Weak to 5=Very Strong)
  • Speaking Skills: Fluency and confidence in spoken communication (1=Very Weak to 5=Very Strong)
  • Writing Skills: Ability to write academic papers and assignments (1=Very Weak to 5=Very Strong)

A composite Proficiency Score was calculated as the mean of the four skill scores for each participant.

4.3 Statistical Analysis Strategy

The analysis followed a systematic approach:

  1. Exploratory data analysis and descriptive statistics
  2. Testing of statistical assumptions (normality, homogeneity of variance)
  3. Hypothesis testing using parametric and non-parametric approaches
  4. Effect size calculation to assess practical significance
  5. Equivalence testing to determine if groups are statistically equivalent
  6. Subgroup analyses by individual skills and level of study

5. Key Calculations and Formulas

To ensure transparency and aid in understanding the analysis, we present the key statistical formulas used in this study:

5.1 Composite Proficiency Score

The overall proficiency score for each student was calculated as:

Proficiency Score = (Reading + Listening + Speaking + Writing) ÷ 4

This equal weighting approach treats all four skills as equally important components of English proficiency.

5.2 Independent Samples t-test

Formula for calculating the t-statistic:

t = (x̄₁ - x̄₂) ÷ √(s²₁/n₁ + s²₂/n₂)

Where:

  • x̄₁, x̄₂ = Mean proficiency scores for Indian and foreign students (3.63 and 3.86)
  • s²₁, s²₂ = Variances of the two groups (0.55 and 0.57)
  • n₁, n₂ = Sample sizes (50 for each group)

Calculation:

t = (3.63 - 3.86) ÷ √(0.55/50 + 0.57/50) = -0.23 ÷ 0.150 = -1.53

With 98 degrees of freedom, this t-value corresponds to p = 0.129, which is greater than α = 0.05.

5.3 Effect Size (Cohen's d)

Formula for calculating Cohen's d:

d = |x̄₁ - x̄₂| ÷ spooled

Where pooled standard deviation is:

spooled = √[((n₁ - 1)s²₁ + (n₂ - 1)s²₂) ÷ (n₁ + n₂ - 2)]

Calculation:

spooled = √[((50-1)×0.55 + (50-1)×0.57) ÷ (50+50-2)] = √[54.74 ÷ 98] = √0.5586 = 0.7475

d = |3.63 - 3.86| ÷ 0.7475 = 0.23 ÷ 0.7475 = 0.3062

This value falls in the "small effect" range (0.2 < d < 0.5).

5.4 Equivalence Testing (TOST Procedure)

The 90% confidence interval for mean difference is calculated as:

CI90% = (x̄₁ - x̄₂) ± tα=0.05,df=98 × SE

Where the standard error (SE) is:

SE = √(s²₁/n₁ + s²₂/n₂)

Calculation:

SE = √(0.55/50 + 0.57/50) = √0.0224 = 0.1502

CI90% = -0.23 ± 1.66 × 0.1502 = -0.23 ± 0.2495 = [-0.4795, 0.0195]

Since this confidence interval is completely contained within our equivalence bounds (±0.5), we can conclude that the groups are statistically equivalent.

5.5 Mann-Whitney U Test

The Mann-Whitney U test is a non-parametric alternative to the t-test that compares distributions rather than means:

U = n₁n₂ + (n₁(n₁+1))/2 - R₁

Where:

  • n₁, n₂ = Sample sizes of the two groups (50 for each)
  • R₁ = Sum of ranks for group 1 (Indian students)

For large samples, the U statistic can be approximated by a normal distribution:

z = (U - (n₁n₂/2)) / √(n₁n₂(n₁+n₂+1)/12)

Result:

U = 1034.5, p = 0.136

The p-value exceeds our threshold of α = 0.05, supporting the conclusion from the t-test.

5.6 Statistical Power Analysis

For our sample size (n₁ = n₂ = 50), α = 0.05, and an effect size d = 0.3062:

Power = 0.389 (38.9%)

The statistical power is the probability of detecting a true effect if one exists. Our study had a moderate power to detect the small effect size observed. This means there was a 38.9% chance of detecting a true difference if one existed.

Note: To achieve 80% power (the conventional target) for detecting an effect size of d = 0.3, approximately 176 students per group would be needed.

5.7 Understanding P-values and Confidence Intervals

Visual explanation of p-value in our context:

The p-value (0.129) represents the probability of observing a difference of -0.23 or more extreme if the null hypothesis were true. Since p > 0.05, we fail to reject the null hypothesis.

Visual explanation of confidence intervals vs. equivalence testing:

The 95% confidence interval (blue) includes zero, indicating no significant difference at α = 0.05. The 90% confidence interval (green) falls entirely within the equivalence bounds (±0.5), allowing us to positively claim statistical equivalence.

9. Hypothesis Testing

We conducted several statistical tests to investigate whether there is a significant difference in English proficiency between Indian and foreign students.

9.1 Research Hypotheses

Null Hypothesis (H0):

There is no significant difference in English language proficiency between Indian and foreign students.

H0: μIndian = μForeign

Alternative Hypothesis (H1):

There is a significant difference in English language proficiency between Indian and foreign students.

H1: μIndian ≠ μForeign

9.2 Independent Samples t-test

Since the assumptions of normality and homogeneity of variances were met, we first conducted an independent samples t-test.

Test t-statistic Degrees of freedom p-value Significant at α=0.05?
Independent Samples t-test -1.5309 98 0.1290 No

The t-test resulted in a p-value of 0.1290, which is greater than the standard alpha level of 0.05. Therefore, we fail to reject the null hypothesis. This suggests that there is no statistically significant difference in English proficiency between Indian and foreign students.

9.3 Mann-Whitney U Test (Non-parametric Alternative)

For robustness, we also conducted the non-parametric Mann-Whitney U test, which does not assume normality.

Test U-statistic p-value Significant at α=0.05?

The Mann-Whitney U test yielded a p-value of 0.1360, which is consistent with the result from the t-test. This further supports the conclusion that there is no statistically significant difference in English proficiency between the two groups.

9.4 One-way ANOVA

Summary of Hypothesis Testing:

All three statistical tests (t-test, Mann-Whitney U, and ANOVA) consistently show p-values greater than the alpha level of 0.05:

11. Equivalence Testing

Traditional hypothesis testing can only tell us whether we can reject the null hypothesis of no difference. However, failing to reject this null doesn't necessarily mean the groups are equivalent. To directly test for equivalence, we conducted the Two One-Sided Tests (TOST) procedure.

11.1 Two One-Sided Tests (TOST) Approach

The TOST procedure involves defining an equivalence margin (±ε) that represents the smallest effect size of practical importance. If the 90% confidence interval for the mean difference falls entirely within this margin, we can conclude that the groups are statistically equivalent.

Measure Value
Mean difference (Indian - Foreign) -0.2300
Standard error of difference 0.1502

90% Confidence interval upper bound 0.0195
Equivalence bounds ±0.5
Equivalence conclusion Groups are equivalent

11.2 Visual Representation of Equivalence Testing

Figure 7: Equivalence testing - 90% confidence interval for mean difference in relation to equivalence bounds

Interpretation of Equivalence Testing:

The 90% confidence interval for the mean difference (-0.4795 to 0.0195) lies entirely within the equivalence bounds of ±0.5. This means that:

  • We can positively assert that the groups are statistically equivalent
  • Any difference between the groups is smaller than what would be considered practically important (less than 0.5 points on a 5-point scale)
  • The equivalence finding strengthens our conclusion beyond simply "failing to reject" the null hypothesis

Conclusion: Based on the equivalence testing, we can confidently state that Indian and foreign students have statistically equivalent levels of English proficiency.

14. Limitations and Future Research

While this study provides valuable insights into English proficiency comparisons between Indian and foreign students, several limitations should be acknowledged when interpreting the results.

14.1 Methodological Limitations

Limitation Description Potential Impact
Self-Reported Data Proficiency scores were self-assessed rather than measured through standardized tests Potential bias due to differing self-evaluation standards across cultural groups
Sample Size The study included 100 participants (50 per group) Limited statistical power to detect small effects
Cross-Sectional Design Data collected at a single point in time Unable to assess proficiency development over time or causal relationships
Heterogeneity of "Foreign Students" The foreign student group included individuals from multiple countries with varying linguistic backgrounds Potential masking of significant differences between specific nationality groups

14.2 Statistical Limitations

  • Statistical Power: The achieved power of 0.65 falls below the conventional target of 0.80, indicating a 35% chance of failing to detect a true medium effect if one exists.
  • Multiple Testing: Multiple statistical tests were conducted without formal correction for family-wise error rate, which could increase the risk of Type I errors.
  • Subgroup Analysis: The sample size becomes smaller when analyzing by level of study or specific skills, reducing statistical power for these comparisons.
  • Measurement Scale: The 5-point Likert scale for proficiency assessment may have limited sensitivity to detect subtle differences between groups.

14.3 Directions for Future Research

Methodological Improvements

  • Include objective proficiency measures (standardized tests)
  • Implement longitudinal design to track proficiency development
  • Increase sample size to improve statistical power
  • Use mixed methods approach incorporating qualitative data

Extended Research Questions

  • Analyze proficiency differences among specific nationality groups
  • Investigate the impact of length of stay on proficiency
  • Examine the role of prior English education background
  • Assess the relationship between proficiency and academic performance

"Despite these limitations, this study provides valuable preliminary evidence regarding English proficiency patterns among Indian and foreign students in higher education contexts. The findings offer a foundation for more comprehensive investigations and targeted language support initiatives."

  • Educators should be aware of potential differences that may emerge at the postgraduate level
  • Peer learning opportunities between Indian and foreign students could be mutually beneficial

15.3 Recommendations

For Language Support Programs:

  1. Speaking Skills Focus: Develop targeted interventions to enhance speaking skills for Indian students, such as conversation clubs, pronunciation workshops, and oral presentation practice.
  2. Postgraduate Support: Implement specialized language support for postgraduate Indian students, particularly those transitioning from undergraduate studies in regional languages.
  3. Peer Learning Initiatives: Create structured opportunities for Indian and foreign students to engage in language exchange activities, leveraging the complementary strengths of each group.
  4. Confidence Building: Develop programs aimed at building confidence in English communication, as self-perceived proficiency often affects actual performance.

For Future Research:

  1. Longitudinal Studies: Track how English proficiency evolves over time during academic study in India, particularly focusing on the undergraduate to postgraduate transition.

Final Conclusion

In response to our initial research question, the evidence strongly supports that there is no significant difference in overall English language proficiency between foreign and Indian students based on country of origin. The groups are statistically equivalent within meaningful bounds.

However, this overall equivalence masks some important nuances: differences exist specifically in speaking skills and at the postgraduate level. These findings suggest that while blanket assumptions about differences in English proficiency are unwarranted, targeted support in specific areas could enhance the academic experience and success of all students.
